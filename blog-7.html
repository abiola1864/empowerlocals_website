LSETF Programme · Agege, Lagos · April 30, 2026

Our hardest bootcamp yet.
Our most important one.

Over 100 entrepreneurs graduated today in Agege. Most had little or no formal education. We had to change everything to reach them — and it changed us too.

← Back to Blog

By Omotola Ogunlana · LSETF Programme · April 30, 2026

Today, 30 April 2026, over 100 small business owners graduated from ELP Foundation's MSME Capacity Building Bootcamp in Agege, Lagos — the fifth session of the year, delivered in partnership with the Lagos State Employment Trust Fund. They sat in rows in maroon and yellow academic gowns. They held certificates. Some took photographs to send to their families.

For many of them, it was the first time they had ever worn an academic gown. The first time anyone had handed them a certificate with their name on it. The first time they had sat in something that felt, even briefly, like a school.

That moment — and the weeks of work that led to it — is the reason we are writing this post. Because this cohort was different from any we have trained before. And because what we learned from them will change how we design every programme we run from here.

What made this cohort different

Every cohort has its own character. Previous Agege groups had a mix of educational backgrounds — some secondary school completers, some with diplomas, a few with degrees. This cohort was different. When we ran our baseline assessment in the first session, we found that the majority of participants had either no formal education or had left school before completing primary level.

Our standard curriculum was not written for them. Not because they were not intelligent — they were extraordinarily intelligent, in the specific way that comes from running a business for ten or fifteen years with no safety net. But our materials assumed a level of literacy in written English that this group did not have. Our exercises asked people to write answers. Our examples used vocabulary that made sense in a training room but not on a market floor.

By the end of session one, our facilitators knew we had a choice. We could continue with the designed curriculum and lose most of the room by session three. Or we could adapt.

"By session two, we had thrown out most of the written exercises. Everything became conversation. Call and response. Demonstration. We spoke in Yoruba, in Pidgin, in whatever the room needed." — Omotola Ogunlana, Training Manager

What adaptation actually looked like

Our facilitators — and here we want to name them specifically, because what they did was not in any training manual — rebuilt the curriculum in real time. Between sessions, they rewrote exercises to be verbal rather than written. They switched from English to Yoruba and Nigerian Pidgin. They replaced abstract financial concepts with direct examples drawn from the specific businesses in the room: the woman selling akara, the man running a recharge card stall, the woman who sold fabric and calculated her margin entirely in her head.

Instead of asking participants to calculate profit on a worksheet, facilitators would stand next to someone at their imaginary stall and walk through the numbers out loud, together. Instead of defining "gross margin," they asked: "If you buy this for two hundred naira and sell it for three hundred, how much did you make? And now — what did you spend to get it to the stall?" The concepts were the same. The pathway was completely different.

The assessment scores reflected the adjustment. The adaptation worked. Participants who had barely engaged in the first session were finishing exercises, contributing to discussions, and describing their own businesses in the language of the curriculum — not because the words changed, but because the examples did.

What they actually came for

Something clarified early in the programme when we asked participants directly why they had enrolled. For many, the honest answer was the LSETF loan. Completing an ELP Foundation bootcamp run in partnership with the Lagos State Employment Trust Fund creates a pathway — not a guarantee, but a documented route — toward accessing a loan from a state institution. For vendors who had been operating informally for years with no access to structured credit, that possibility was significant enough to bring them through the door five Thursdays in a row.

We do not see this as a problem. The loan motivation is legitimate, and the people pursuing it deserve that pathway. What we observed is that something shifted over the course of the sessions. By week three, participants were arriving early. By week five, a number of them were asking whether there was a continuation. The loan had brought them in. The learning had given them a reason to stay.

That combination — practical aspiration meeting genuine learning — is what we want every cohort to feel. This group made it visible more clearly than any we have run before.

The moment that stopped the room

In session four, our facilitator asked participants to share one thing they had always wanted to do with their business — not what was realistic, not what they had the money for, but what they had dreamed about. The question was not in the original curriculum. She asked it because the room had reached the kind of trust that makes honest answers possible.

What followed surprised everyone, including us. A woman who sold second-hand clothes said she had always wanted to open a proper boutique — with a fitting room and a mirror where her customers could see themselves. A man who sold phone accessories said he wanted to build a repair training centre for young people in his street. A provision seller said she wanted her shop to have a sign with her name on it — not the business name, her own name.

These were not business plans. They were statements of dignity. They were what happens when someone who has been running a business for fifteen years, largely invisible to formal economic systems, is given a room, a facilitator, and five sessions of being taken seriously.

Kudos to the facilitators

We want to be direct about this: the success of this cohort belongs to the facilitators. The curriculum gave them a starting point. The adapatation, the language-switching, the on-the-spot restructuring of exercises, the patience to repeat a concept five times in five different ways until the room understood — that was entirely them. Training is easy when participants match the assumptions your curriculum was built around. Training is hard when they don't. Our team did the hard version, without complaint, every Thursday for five weeks.

On working with political leaders — and staying independent

This bootcamp brought us face to face with something we want to be honest about: the complexity of operating in a space where political actors are also present. The local government chairman donated to the event. That support was appreciated and made the graduation possible at the scale it reached. But it also raised a question we take seriously — how do we remain genuinely independent in our teaching, our assessments, and our decisions about who benefits, when political leaders are part of the room?

Our answer, worked out over several years of operating in Lagos, is that political engagement and pedagogical independence are not mutually exclusive — but they require active effort to keep separate. We welcome support from government and elected officials. We believe that part of our work is shaping how political leaders understand local businesses and what they actually need. If a local government chairman leaves a graduation with a clearer picture of what micro-entrepreneurs face, that is influence we are glad to have. What we do not do is adjust who gets trained, what gets taught, or how results are reported based on who contributed to the event.

The ability to work alongside government and political leaders — while maintaining the independence of our teaching — is something we have come to see as a core part of what ELP Foundation does. We are not a campaign. We are not a contractor. We are a foundation that trains people, measures what it does, and publishes what it finds. That independence is worth protecting, and this cohort reminded us why.

What we are changing

We are revising the LSETF curriculum before the next cohort in June 2026. The revisions will formally incorporate what this group taught us: more verbal exercises, Pidgin and Yoruba language options for all core concepts, facilitator discretion to adjust the pace and method per session based on the room, and a stronger emphasis on the dreaming exercise in session four.

We are also adding a question to our baseline survey: not just education level, but primary language of business communication. We should have been asking this from the start.

The next cohort opens in June 2026. Applications go through the contact page or via LSETF directly. If you run a community organisation, market association, or local government programme in Lagos and want to bring the bootcamp to your area, we want to hear from you.

Are Small Businesses
Actually Using Tech?

We surveyed 1,500 businesses across 40 Lagos markets. What we found challenged every assumption we had brought into the field.

← Back to Blog

By Akosile Oluwayomi and Oyebanjo Abiola · January 31, 2024 · 8 min read

Read on Medium →
"We are not the market women, we are the market women's best friend."

This saying circulates on Nigerian social media as a joke. But when we ran a quick quiz at our December 2023 mini-summit in Lagos — asking over 40 attendees whether they had a family member or close friend selling in a Lagos open market — almost every hand went up. The connection to local small business is not peripheral for most Nigerians. It is personal, and close.

That closeness is why ELP Foundation does what it does. And it is why, in 2023, we decided to go beyond our own programme experience and ask a harder question: are the small businesses we work alongside actually using technology? Not in theory. Not in aspirational policy documents. In practice — on the market floor, in the afternoon heat, between customers.

What we found contradicted most of what we had assumed going in.

The numbers did not add up

The common assumption among development practitioners is that technology adoption among small firms is growing — that the availability of record-keeping apps, mobile payment tools, and inventory software means that at least some portion of the market has quietly started using them. We held some version of that assumption ourselves.

Then we went into the field. Over four years of survey work, including a wave covering more than 1,500 small businesses across 40 markets in Lagos State, we looked specifically for firms that had adopted digital record-keeping tools — apps designed to help them log sales, track stock, and understand their margins. We expected to find a meaningful cluster. We found almost none. Across 1,500 businesses, we could count on one hand the firms that had genuinely adopted a digital record-keeping tool and were using it consistently.

One statistic gave us pause, and eventually gave us hope: over 90% of those same business owners owned and regularly used an internet-enabled smartphone. The gap was not infrastructure. It was not access. Something else was stopping adoption.

Why they are not adopting — five lessons from our summit

In December 2023, ELP Foundation convened a mini-summit on technology for small businesses — bringing together representatives from the Lagos State Employment Trust Fund (LSETF), Growing Businesses Foundation, Fate Foundation, 8th Gear Hub, Lagos Food Bank Initiative, and small firm owners themselves. The conversation produced five clear lessons.

1

Give it time

In 2000, Nigeria's internet penetration was 0.3%. By 2021 it was over 55%. Adoption curves do not move in straight lines, and small business technology adoption will follow a similar trajectory — but only if stakeholders remain consistent, patient, and present. The instinct to measure impact in one or two years misreads how behaviour change actually works.

2

Treat technology as a tool, not a solution

Most technology built for small business was not designed with Lagos market vendors in mind. It was designed for a user who is literate, has consistent data access, and has time to learn a new interface. When technology is sophisticated rather than useful, small firms do not adopt it — and blaming the firms misses the point entirely. The design needs to change, not the user.

3

Communicate the cost of not adopting

Telling a market vendor about the benefits of digital records is less persuasive than telling them what they are losing by not keeping them. One insight from the summit: framing it as "Firm X across the road earns twelve naira more per transaction because they record their sales" lands differently than any abstract pitch about technology. Loss framing works. Aspiration framing often does not, at this stage.

4

Build for continuous use, not just first adoption

Initial adoption is not the goal — sustained use is. Most technology interventions celebrate when a firm downloads an app or attends a training session. The harder question is whether they are still using it six months later. Technology that is expensive to maintain, hard to navigate without help, or that solves a problem the vendor does not actually feel as a problem will not survive first contact with a busy trading day.

5

Meet them where they already are

90% of our survey respondents already had smartphones with internet access. The infrastructure gap is narrower than assumed. The more immediate gap is in trusted relationships — the kind that make a vendor willing to try something new with their livelihood. ELP Foundation's experience, across years of working in Lagos markets, is that the relationship precedes the tool. You have to be in the market first — and you have to stay.

What we are doing about it

These findings directly shaped ELP Foundation's programming choices in 2024 and 2025. EmpowerBot — our WhatsApp-native AI coaching tool — was built specifically because 90% of our survey respondents already had WhatsApp. We did not build a new app. We went to where they already were.

Our Local Business School curriculum was rewritten after this research to reduce reliance on written exercises, increase verbal interaction, and anchor every concept to a real business situation in the room. The SME Digital RecordKeeping Report was produced as a printed resource specifically for vendors who are not regular users of digital content.

The research continues. We are currently measuring the causal effect of our digital record-keeping intervention on firm revenue and survival — a randomised evaluation that will give us harder numbers on what actually works, not just what we believe works. Partners on that work include CEGA, IGC, and PEDL.

The connection to local small business is not peripheral for most Nigerians. It is personal. That is why the question of whether technology is helping or not deserves more honest answers than we have been giving it.

Research · 2022

We wrote a 98-page handbook
for small businesses. Here's why.

Most small business training fails because it is not specific enough. The SME Digest was our attempt to change that.

← Back to Blog

By Abiola Oyebanjo · December 2022

In 2022, after three years of surveys across Lagos markets, we kept running into the same problem. Entrepreneurs came to training sessions, participated actively, went back to their businesses — and changed very little. Not because they were not motivated. But because what they were taught was generic. A module on "financial management" that applied to a large exporting firm was being delivered, with minor adjustments, to a woman selling provisions from a roadside stall.

The gap we set out to close

Our 2022 survey of nearly 2,000 small business owners in Lagos showed that 70 to 90 percent were still relying on paper records — not because they rejected technology, but because no one had ever shown them a system that worked for their specific kind of business. 98.4 percent owned a mobile phone. Over 75 percent said they were open to learning better record-keeping. The knowledge and willingness were there. The right material was not.

Seven in ten business owners said that training on record-keeping is necessary for running their business. We decided to build what they were asking for.

What the SME Digest is

The SME Digest is a 98-page handbook designed specifically for micro and small business owners in Nigeria. It covers the basics — bookkeeping, pricing, profit calculation — but it goes further than most training materials do. It explains why you should pay yourself a salary as a business owner. It walks through the difference between revenue and profit in language that does not require a finance degree. It has sections on digital tools, tax basics, and how to prepare your business for a loan application.

Every section was written based on what we actually observed in our surveys. The chapter on record-keeping, for example, addresses the specific reason our data showed entrepreneurs most commonly fail to keep records: they do not trust that the benefit will outweigh the time. So instead of telling people they must keep records, it starts with a case for why records serve their specific interests — not a bank's, not a regulator's.

How it was used

The Digest was distributed during our 2022 Market Outreach Programme across Lagos — a programme that was covered by Nigerian Tribune and ThisDay newspaper. Volunteers visited markets in Agege, Ikorodu, Epe, and across the Lagos mainland, working directly with business owners to introduce the handbook and answer questions on the spot. The sessions were structured around the Digest chapters, not the other way around.

We also shared it digitally through WhatsApp — the channel most of our beneficiaries use daily. Business owners could refer back to it anytime, without needing an internet connection after the first download.

What the press said

The December 2022 outreach was covered by two national newspapers. Nigerian Tribune reported that the Foundation was "equipping SMEs with business insights and digital training" through a combination of field workshops and the Digest. ThisDay described the programme as a practical intervention that went beyond traditional NGO training models by combining evidence from research with on-the-ground delivery.

Both reports highlighted something we had designed deliberately: the outreach was not a one-day event. The Digest was the follow-up material — something participants could take home and use long after the training team had left the market.

Download it free

The SME Digest is available free of charge. It has no commercial purpose. If you know a small business owner who could benefit from it, share it. If you run an NGO, a government agency, or a development programme working with small firms in Nigeria, you are welcome to use it — just credit ELP Foundation.

What 250 Entrepreneurs in Agege
Taught Us About Financial Literacy

We tested participants before and after five sessions. The results changed how we design training.

← Back to Blog

By Omotola Ogunlana · February 18, 2026

Financial literacy training is everywhere in Lagos. Government agencies run it. NGOs run it. Banks run it. The sessions are usually full, the participants are usually engaged, and the certificates are usually printed. What rarely happens is a measurement of whether anyone actually learned anything — or changed any behaviour as a result.

When ELP Foundation partnered with the Lagos State Employment Trust Fund to deliver the 2026 Agege MSME Bootcamp, we decided to do things differently. Every participant completed a standardised knowledge test at the start of the first session and again at the end of the final session. We wanted to know: what did 250 entrepreneurs in Agege actually walk away knowing that they did not know before?

Session one: the shock of the baseline

The first session began with a ten-question assessment on business fundamentals — cost of goods sold, breakeven pricing, simple bookkeeping. The average score was 38%. That is not a reflection of intelligence or effort. It reflects that almost none of these topics are taught formally in Nigerian secondary schools, and most of these entrepreneurs learned their trade by watching and doing.

What struck our trainers was not the score but the confidence. Most participants answered the baseline questions quickly and without hesitation. They were not guessing — they genuinely believed their answers were correct. This is a well-documented phenomenon in adult learning: people who have been doing something for years develop strong intuitions that feel like knowledge but may not align with technical definitions.

"I have been selling for eight years. I thought I knew what profit meant. After session two, I realised I had been calculating it wrong the whole time." — Participant, Agege Bootcamp 2026

What the five sessions focused on

Over five consecutive Thursdays, we moved from fundamentals to application. Session one covered recordkeeping and the difference between revenue and profit. Session two focused on pricing — specifically the gap between what something costs to produce and what the market will pay. Session three introduced digital tools, including EmpowerBot. Session four covered credit literacy and LSETF loan products. Session five brought participants back to present a one-page business plan using the tools from the previous four sessions.

We deliberately kept each session under three hours and hands-on. Every concept was illustrated with examples drawn from the businesses in the room — a bread seller's costing, a tailor's pricing model, a provisions shop's stock management. Abstract finance became concrete when it was about someone's actual business.

The endline results

At the end of session five, participants completed the same ten-question assessment from session one. The average score rose from 38% to 71% — a 33 percentage-point increase. Ninety-six percent of participants said they would recommend the programme to another entrepreneur. More importantly, 61% reported that they had already made at least one specific change to how they managed their business during the five-week period.

We are still collecting the 30-day follow-up data — phone-based check-ins to see which changes persisted. That data will be published in the programme report available in the Downloads section of this site.

What this changes about how we design training

The Agege results confirmed two things we suspected but could not prove before. First: training works when it is specific, contextualised, and repeated over multiple sessions — not delivered in a single day. Second: measuring learning creates accountability in both directions. Trainers work harder when they know their participants will be tested. Participants engage more seriously when they know their understanding will be assessed, not just their attendance.

The next cohort is being designed now. We are looking for communities and funding partners. If you work in local government, development finance, or corporate social responsibility, we want to talk.

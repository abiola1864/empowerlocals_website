From 4 Bags to 10: The Minna Farmers
Who Changed Everything

We gave 11 farmers certified seeds and trust. Here is what happened when the rains came.

← Back to Blog

By empowerFarmers Team · March 2, 2026

By any conventional measure, Habiba should not have qualified for agricultural support. She had no bank account, no title deed on her land, and no credit history. She had never bought certified seeds. Every season, she planted the same local saved maize she had kept from the previous harvest, on a half-acre plot in Garatu community, Minna. Every season, the yield barely fed her family for four months.

In April 2024, empowerFarmers enrolled Habiba in our pilot programme. She received SC651 certified hybrid seeds, two bags of NPK 20-10-10 fertilizer, neem-coated urea, herbicide, and a labour cash grant to hire help clearing her plot. She paid nothing upfront. There was no loan. No interest. No collateral. Just trust — and a clear expectation: at harvest, she would contribute five mudus of grain back into the community fund.

"We had never seen cobs this size." — Farmer, Garatu community, after the 2024 harvest

What the numbers looked like before

Before the programme, the average harvest across our 11 enrolled farmers was approximately 4 bags — roughly 200 kilograms of maize per half-acre. That is near the bottom of what that land is capable of producing. Local saved seed loses vigour over generations. Without the right fertilizer applied at the right time, soil nutrients deplete. And without herbicide in the critical first ten weeks, weeds compete aggressively with young maize.

These are not problems caused by poor farming. They are problems caused by the absence of inputs that the land needs and the farmer cannot afford to buy upfront.

What happened when the rains came

By October 2024, the harvest data was in. Across all 11 farmers, the average yield had reached 8 to 10 bags — roughly 400 to 500 kilograms of maize. That is a three-fold increase on the same land, in the same season, with the same farmers. Nine of the eleven described it as their best harvest in living memory. None reported crop failure.

More tellingly: all eleven contributed their grain back to the community fund. Not because they were contractually obliged — there is no legal agreement. But because the harvest was real enough that giving back five mudus felt painless. One farmer contributed more than required and asked if he could join the programme again next season.

The model that makes this work

empowerFarmers calls this the Patience Model. We wait for the harvest. Inputs are delivered before planting, not after. The farmer's contribution comes at harvest — six months later. There is no pressure during the growing season, no loan officer calling, no interest accumulating. The incentive to succeed is built into the farmer's own interest: a better harvest means more food for their family and a smaller proportional contribution.

In 2026, we are scaling to 100 farmers across Garatu and Taidna communities. We have visited both communities, spoken with the community leaders, and collected baseline data. Farmers have confirmed participation. The planting window opens May 2026. Eighty-five spots are still open for anyone who wants to fund a farmer.

You do not need to be an agricultural expert. You do not need to be wealthy. At roughly $60 for a half-acre package, one person can change one family's harvest — and through the community fund, help the next family too.

Research · 2019–2023

We went to 100 markets
before we taught anyone anything.

Before writing a handbook, before building a bot, before training a single entrepreneur — we listened. Here is what three years of Lagos market surveys taught us.

← Back to Blog

By Abiola Oyebanjo · Research Team · 2023

The man in the photo sits in a wooden kiosk in Lagos. Above his counter: money transfer, withdrawal, DSTV subscription, airtime top-up, bank account opening. He runs every financial service his neighbourhood needs. He has been there for years. He knows every face, every name, every regular. But if you asked him to show you his records for last month, he would tap his temple. They are all in there.

We spent three years — 2019, 2022, and 2023 — visiting markets like his across Lagos. 101 markets. 15 local councils. Nearly 2,000 business owners. We asked questions. We watched. We surveyed. We came back. And what we found changed how we designed every programme we have run since.

The numbers that stopped us

98.4 percent of the small business owners we met owned a mobile phone. 77.8 percent used internet-enabled applications. Over 75 percent said they were open to learning better record-keeping methods. By any conventional measure, these were technology-ready people.

And yet: 70 to 90 percent were still keeping all their business records on paper — or in their heads. Not one of the vendors we surveyed in 2019 used a third-party bookkeeping app or consultant. Not one.

Technology readiness is not the same as technology adoption. The gap between them is where most NGO programmes fail.

What she told us at her stall

The woman in the second photo sells plantains and bananas. She has been at the same spot for over a decade. She knew her best-selling days, her slow weeks, which supplier gave the freshest stock. But she had never separated her business money from her personal money. She had never paid herself a salary. She did not know her profit margin — not because she was unsupported, but because no one had ever explained why any of that mattered in a way that connected to her actual life.

That conversation — repeated hundreds of times across Lagos markets — is why ELP Foundation exists. Not to introduce technology. Not to run training for training's sake. But to understand first, and design second.

What the data told us about why people don't change

Our surveys identified four types of costs that stop small business owners from adopting digital records. The mental effort of learning something new. The attention it demands during a busy trading day. The question of whether it is actually worth it for their specific business. And the time it takes to build a new habit.

What we found was that when any of these costs was too high — or when the benefit was not explained clearly enough — businesses dropped out. Not because they were resistant, but because no one had made the case in terms they recognised as relevant. This is what motivated the SME Digest. And later, EmpowerBot.

From the streets to the classroom to the farm

The survey data shaped everything. The Digest chapters were ordered by what our respondents said they needed most — stock management first, then pricing, then profit calculation. The EmpowerBot training modules were built from the questions market vendors actually asked. The LSETF bootcamp sessions were structured around the specific gaps our data showed participants had at baseline.

And when we moved to EmpowerFarmers in Minna — a completely different context, different crop, different community — we still started the same way. Survey first. Community engagement first. Evidence first.

If you want to change behaviour, you have to understand it first. We went to 100 markets before we taught anyone anything. We think that is still the right order.
